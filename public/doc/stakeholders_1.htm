Stakeholders

Every goal has a set of stakeholders, defined as all those who are affected by and/or can affect the achievement of the goal.

When building a map, existing stakeholders repeatedly ask whether there are other stakeholders who have not yet been recognised. They continue to do this throughout the life of the map, with the aim of ensuring that all stakeholders are identified. Note that stakeholders include not just people from the organisation engaged in the change initiative, but also people from external organisations, including customers, partners and suppliers. They might also include, say, representatives of a regulatory body or the general public.

It is the stakeholders—collectively—who create the map, own the map and deliver the map. All stakeholders are responsible for playing an active part in this process. They must:

•invest time and effort

•participate actively in discussions around the map

•speak freely and openly

•respect other stakeholders

•listen and consider

•challenge constructively

•and contribute their own knowledge and insights.

All the stakeholders in a given goal share some responsibility for that goal. And all stakeholders in any goal share some responsibility for the map as a whole.

All stakeholders are equal—every opinion carries as much weight as every other. Decisions are not taken by majority vote but rather require consensus.

Every stakeholder set includes four distinguished stakeholders with special responsibilities:

The Goal Manager

•maintains the goal definition to reflect the consolidated views of all stakeholders

•maintains the goal status so that it is always accurate up-to-date

•coordinates and oversees all activities needed to achieve the goal

•has overall responsibility for ensuring that the goal is delivered

•must have the authority to match this responsibility.

The Goal Champion

•acts as an advocate for the goal, certainly to stakeholders of other goals and perhaps within the wider organisation

•protects the resources assigned for achieving the goal (so that they are not diverted to other ends)

•has the ‘deciding voice’ if other stakeholders have disagreements that cannot be resolved

•must command the respect of other stakeholders.

The Risk Manager

•coordinates identification of the risks that impact the goal, and of avoidance and mitigation measures

•maintains the definition of each risk

•maintains the status of each risk so that it is always accurate and up-to-date

•ensures that other stakeholders are informed of any changes in risk status

•ensures that the Goal Manager initiates and coordinates avoidance and mitigation measures as appropriate.

The Change Manager

•supervises all changes to the goal definition once the goal has been placed under change control

•ensures that all stakeholders who might be impacted are consulted before the change is made—especially the Goal Managers of all supported goals.

Beyond these four, a goal can have any number of other stakeholders.

In some cases, a stake may be held by a group rather than a single individual—such as customers or call centre staff or the general public. In this case, one person acts as the representative for the group and is responsible for ensuring that the group’s views are properly presented.

Just as each goal has its own set of stakeholders, the overall map also has its own set. Then:

•any newly-created top-level goal inherits all stakeholders from the overall map

•any newly-created sub-goal inherits all stakeholders from its parent goal.

In both cases the inheritance is by one-off copy at the time of goal creation. Subsequently, changing either stakeholder set—the one from which the copy was taken, or the newly-created copy—does not affect the other.
