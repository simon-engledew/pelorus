Risk management

Risks are managed at each individual goal. Each goal has a Risk Manager who takes the lead, but every stakeholder in the goal shares some responsibility for risk management.

For purposes of risk identification, stakeholders simply assume that their supporting goals will deliver as promised and then ask what might now prevent us from delivering our goal?

At the basic level, the stakeholders give each identified risk a name and a description. They also give the risk a traffic-light status based on their subjective judgement of its severity. Any risks with amber or red status are then subject to close monitoring.

At the advanced level, the stakeholders examine each risk in more detail. Having identified a risk, they ask what is the probability that this risk will actually materialise? They assign a base probability in the range 1 to 5, where 1 is highly unlikely and 5 is highly likely. Similarly they assess the impact that the risk would have if it actually did materialise and assign a base impact in the range 1 to 5, where 1 is minor impact and 5 is massive impact (i.e. would totally prevent achievement of the goal). They then multiply the base probability and base impact to give a base severity—a number in the range 1 to 25.

When judged appropriate—and always when the base severity is 5 or more—the stakeholders should look for avoidance and mitigation measures. An avoidance measure is one that will reduce the probability of the risk materialising. A mitigation measure is one that will reduce the impact of the risk if it should materialise.

Having identified avoidance and mitigation measures, the stakeholders assess the adjusted probability of the risk and the adjusted impact—the probability and impact with the avoidance and mitigation measures in place (and under the assumption that these measures are enacted as and when appropriate). Both adjusted probability and adjusted impact are numbers in the range 0 to 5. They then multiply these two numbers to give the adjusted severity, a number in the range 0 to 25.

The traffic-light status of the risk is then governed by the adjusted severity:

•if the adjusted severity is ≥ 15, the status is red

•if the adjusted severity is between 5 and 14 (inclusive), the status is amber

•if the adjusted severity is <5, the status is green.

The stakeholders identify as many avoidance and mitigation measures as possible, so as to drive the adjusted severity as low as possible. Ideally they should drive it to less than 5 so that the risk is green.

Even where each individual risk at a goal has green status, a combination of several low-severity risks may be such as to cause concern. The combination should then be treated as a separate risk in its own right.

The Risk Manager is responsible for continued monitoring of all risks, ensuring stakeholders are informed of any perceived changes in adjusted severity and notifying the Goal Manager of the need to enact avoidance or mitigation measures. The Goal Manager is then responsible for initiating and managing the enactment.
