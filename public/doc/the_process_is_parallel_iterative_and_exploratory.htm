The process is parallel, iterative and exploratory

Any ‘serial’ presentation of Pelorus—such as that given in this Guide—may leave the impression that the process is sequential and linear, steadily progressing step-by-step. Such an impression is highly misleading. In practice, and unavoidably, the process is highly parallel and highly iterative. At any given time, many stakeholders are focusing on the map, many questions are being asked and decisions being made, and old questions and decisions are being revisited. Some stakeholders may be extending and refining one area of the map while others may be reconsidering and revising other areas.

This all relates to one of the central principles of Pelorus, that of learning, adjustment and convergence. It is not the case that a map is first defined in its entirety and then followed ‘mechanically’ so as to traverse the specified change paths and reach some fixed set of benefits. Rather, during both map definition and delivery the stakeholders will be continually learning—about what is feasible or infeasible, what fits together and aligns, what looks promising or unpromising, what has worked or not worked, what has changed in the external environment, and so on. Consequently they will be making frequent changes, often minor but sometimes major, to both the body goals that represent the change paths and the head goals that represent the business benefits. So definition and delivery of a map is not a matter of ‘laying rails and then following them’. Rather it is a process of continual learning, adjustment, convergence and interception. Ultimately, the stakeholders do not arrive at a place that was fixed from the outset, but rather at one where they are happy to be in the light of what they have learned along the way.
