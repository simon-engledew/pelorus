The process

The process of creating and delivering a Pelorus map has four phases: Concept, Definition, Delivery and Harvesting.

•The Concept phase creates a ‘skeleton’ map that gives an overview of the proposed change, covering both the change paths and the business benefits.

•The Definition phase populates this skeleton map, taking it to a level of precision and detail that meets the needs of all stakeholders.

•The Delivery phase gradually achieves most of the goals in the map—those that represent the change paths.

•The Harvesting phase achieves the head goals in the map—those that represent the business benefits.

Rather than being precisely defined, the boundaries between the phases are fuzzy. For example, there can be no notion of waiting until the map is ‘finished’ before commencing the Delivery phase, because the map always remains dynamic and in a sense provisional. It does not represent a fixed plan to be followed mechanically but rather a basis for conversation, incremental progress, feedback and learning.

So the phases do not represent a strict discipline that must be followed religiously, but rather act as a broad guide to how concerns evolve over the lifetime of a map. The stakeholders might agree, say, that “we’re beginning to turn our attention from concept to definition”, but that is something that happens over time rather than at one specific point, and perhaps sooner for some stakeholders than others.

Nevertheless, optionally, the stakeholders may hold a Gateway review at each transition. This does not affect the basic nature of the process—it remains fluid and, rather than being a point event, the transition still takes some period of time. Any Gateway review is held at some convenient point within that period.

Throughout the four phases, all stakeholders must continually ask and discuss a large number of questions, relating both to the map as a whole and to the specific goals in which they hold a stake.

Finally, it is worth repeating a warning from the Principles section. Any ‘serial’ presentation of the process —such as that given here—may leave the impression that it is sequential and linear. Such an impression is highly misleading. In practice, and unavoidably, the process is highly parallel and highly iterative. Throughout it is underpinned by continual conversations amongst the stakeholders, continual questioning and continual challenging.
