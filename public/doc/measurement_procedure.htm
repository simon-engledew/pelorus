Measurement procedure

For defining the way in which a factor is measured, Pelorus uses operational definitions. With this approach, there is no concept of a factor having both a definition and a separate measurement procedure. Rather, there is only a measurement procedure and the factor is actually defined by the results of this procedure—see the panel below.

The procedure must of course be practical. Ideally it should be repeatable by anyone at any time and should be entirely objective—the result should not be dependent upon the person(s) who perform the procedure.

However, the key requirement is that the procedure must be agreed by all stakeholders. In some contexts they may accept a rather ‘casual’ procedure, while in other cases they may insist on rigour. But in any event, the agreed procedure is the definition of the factor and cannot be subverted.

 

Operational Definitions

With operational definitions there is no concept of having both a definition and a measurement procedure. Rather there is only a measurement procedure which serves as the definition.

Take as an example a factor concerned with the usability of an IT system such as the new CRM system that features in our example map. Usability is a notion that frequently leads to lengthy discussion and argument. It would be possible to first attempt to define what we mean by usable—perhaps using words such as intuitive, friendly, error-tolerant and so on—and then to define a procedure for determining whether a given system was indeed usable according to our definition.

The difficulty here of course is the room for disagreement about whether the measurement procedure accurately measures usability as we have defined it. An operational definition cuts through this dilemma by omitting the separate definition and having only the measurement procedure. So the stakeholders might agree the following measurement procedure for usability:

•Identify 10 individual members of the call centre staff who (i) have at least 2 years experience with Windows-based software; (ii) have completed and passed the 2-day training course; (iii) have at least three weeks continuous experience of using the system as part of their normal working routine.

•Monitor the work of these individuals over a period of 3 days.

•Determine (i) the total number of error-free system transactions completed by these 10 individuals over the 3 day period; (ii) the total number of transactions with errors.

•The system is usable if and only if the total number of error-free transactions is >1000 and the total number of error transactions <20.

Once this measurement procedure has been agreed, it actually defines what the stakeholders mean by usability. It is not legitimate for an observer to subsequently challenge the system’s usability on the grounds of awkward interaction sequences, poor screen layout, or whatever—those issues simply do not feature in the definition as agreed. Conversely, it is not legitimate to claim that the system is usable if it fails by this procedure, regardless of any arguments of ergonomics, a fancy help system or whatever. This procedure is the stakeholders’ definition of usability and cannot be subverted.
