Delivery Phase

Objective

To progressively achieve all the goals in the map that effect the business change.

Actions

1.Throughout the phase, continue the questions and actions of the Definition phase so as to maintain a map that

•is valid, complete and aligned

•reflects the individual knowledge and collective wisdom of all stakeholders

•reflects what the stakeholders have learned thus far from definition and delivery of the map.

2.Create initial plans of the activities needed to achieve each goal. Subsequently, revise and refine these plans as possible and appropriate—see Three-level planning.

3.As possible and appropriate, carry out the activities needed to achieve each goal.

4.Throughout the phase, for each goal, provide predicted/actual values for all factors—see Notes below. Each predicted/actual value may impact the traffic-light status of the factor and hence the status of the goal.

5.Throughout the phase, manage the risks at each goal—see Risk Management. These management activities may impact the traffic-light status of a risk and hence the status of the goal.

6.Whenever a potential or actual problem is identified at a goal—typically through its traffic-light status going amber or red—take appropriate action. The options are:

•Deferment. The stakeholders in the map can elect to do nothing for the moment, but simply wait and see if the situation at the problem goal can be improved.

•Containment. The stakeholders can decide that the problem should be contained within a specific area of the map so as to avoid any impact on the head goals that represent the business benefits. Depending on the nature and scale of the problem, the containment boundary could be at the problem goal itself or further up the change paths (i.e. at appropriate points in the chains of goals that the problem goal supports, either directly or indirectly).

•Adjustment. The stakeholders adjust the map so as to restore it to harmony. The adjustments might include the addition or removal of goals and factors, and changes to factor target values. Adjustments can be made to any parts of the map, including goals that represent the business benefits.

7.If appropriate, at any time, make the collective decision that the change initiative should be abandoned. In this case, explore the two questions of what have we learned? and where do we go from here?

8.Once all goals that effect the business change have been achieved, proceed to the Harvesting phase.

Notes

For providing predicted/actual factor values at a goal (action 4 above), there are two options:

•One option is to wait for a major milestone, such as actually achieving the goal or exhausting one of the resources assigned to the goal (e.g. Goal achievement date). At this point the stakeholders can provide predicted/actual values that essentially show actual performance against targets.

•The preferred option is to provide predicted values for all factors throughout the Definition phase, either at regular intervals—such as weekly or fortnightly—or whenever the predicted values change. People are familiar with providing regular predictions for resources factors—such as the remaining time needed to complete an activity, or the total cost of achieving a goal—but are less familiar with providing predictions for results factors. However, similar principles apply. Of course, at some point the supplied values cease to be predictions and are instead the actual measured values.

The primary advantage of the second option is that it gives all stakeholders early warning of any potential problem, rather than delaying the warning until the problem has been confirmed.
