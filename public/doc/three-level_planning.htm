Three-level planning

For planning the delivery of a map, the preferred approach is to employ three levels. Level 1 plans are maintained within the Pelorus software, while level 2 and 3 plans are held in other tools.

Level 1—Target values for resource factors

The level 1 plan takes the form of target values for the key resource factors of all goals. During the Concept and Definition phases, the stakeholders (led by the Goal Managers) identify these key resource factors, such as Goal achievement date and Goal budget, and provide initial target values.

All stakeholders must accept that these values are provisional—they are simply best estimates based on what is currently known. Subsequently, as with any other factors, the values are adjusted and refined as appropriate throughout the Definition and Delivery phases to reflect what stakeholders have learned. And a single-valued target might be refined to a three-valued target.

In many cases, the provision of a target value for a resource factor will require a top-level analysis of how the resource will be consumed. For example, with a goal achievement date, this analysis would consist of identifying the major activities needed to achieve the goal and the elapsed time required for each. In such cases, the analysis is carried forward to level 2 planning.

The target values for some resource factors at a goal may depend on the target values for resource factors at other goals. The most obvious example is with goal achievement date—the target value for this factor at a given goal will typically depend on the target values for the corresponding factor at all its supporting goals (i.e. typically, a goal cannot be achieved until some time after all its supporting goals have been achieved). Given these dependencies, the level 1 plan is essentially a single plan for the entire map, developed through stakeholder collaboration.

Level 2—Outline resource plans

Level 2 plans take the form of outline plans for the use of the various resources. For example, they typically include plans that show the activities required for achieving goals and the timing of these activities (i.e. Gantt charts or similar). Being outline, these plans show only top-level activities, with no decomposition into sub-activities. Equally, there might be other outline plans showing the use of goal budgets or the roles of people assigned to work on goals.

The scope of level 2 plans can vary from one change initiative to another, depending on the structure of the change and the specific resource. The options are:

•a single plan covering the whole map

•a number of plans, each covering one area of the map (i.e. a few related goals)

•a dedicated plan for each top-level goal.

As with level 1 plans, level 2 plans remain provisional. They are adjusted and refined throughout the Definition and Delivery phases to reflect what stakeholders have learned.

Level 3—Detailed resource plans

The preferred approach to delivering goals is through a series of timeboxes—short time periods of strictly fixed duration (such as two weeks). During each timebox, the people assigned to work on a goal complete some number of defined tasks towards achievement of the goal. Level 3 plans cover these timeboxes.

Drawing on the level 1 and level 2 plans and led by the Goal Manager, the stakeholders plan each timebox on a just in time basis—that is, they produce the plans for each timebox at the end of the previous timebox, just before the new one is about to start.

As a minimum, there will be a task plan for the timebox, showing the tasks to be undertaken. However, the fixed duration of the timebox must be balanced by some flexibility in the tasks to be performed. So, for example, some tasks may be mandatory for the timebox while others may be optional, to be performed if time allows.

Depending on stakeholder preferences, the task plan might take the form of a simple list of tasks to be performed as those working on the task see fit. Alternatively, it could take the form of a Gantt chart or similar.

In addition to the task plan, a timebox might also have plans for the use of other resources, including both money and people.

Given their nature, level 3 plans are local to an individual goal. On occasion it may be convenient to synchronise the timeboxes for a group of related goals, but this is a matter for local decision rather than a general requirement.

At the end of each timebox, the stakeholders should revisit the level 1 and level 2 plans and when necessary update them in the light of what has been learned.
