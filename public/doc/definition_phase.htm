Definition Phase

Objective

To fully populate the skeleton map that was carried forward from the Concept phase. This fully-populated map must be sufficiently precise and detailed to:

•allow all stakeholders to know precisely what each goal is promising to deliver

The stakeholders in a goal must know precisely what they are responsible for delivering and equally must know what they can expect from their supporting goals. The definition of each goal—its sub-goals and factors—represents a ‘contract’ that must be complete, precise and detailed enough to meet the needs of all parties.

•give all stakeholders sufficient confidence that risks are being managed

Stakeholders need to be confident that risks have been identified and properly assessed, and that the severity of risks is being contained through appropriate avoidance and mitigation measures.

The populated map should properly reflect the individual knowledge and collective wisdom of all stakeholders.

One possible outcome of the phase is a decision that the change should not be pursued.

Actions

1.For each goal in the map, repeatedly ask four questions:

•Who are the stakeholders in this goal?

Edit existing stakes and add new stakes as appropriate.

•What precisely is this goal?

If necessary, edit the goal name and/or description to clarify the stakeholders’ improved understanding. Create any sub-goals. Edit existing factors, and create new factors, as necessary to fully define the goal and provide a complete ‘contract’ including both the results factors and the resources factors. Whenever there is any doubt as to which of the two is more appropriate, always give an advanced-level (three-valued) factor definition rather than a basic-level (single-valued) definition.

•Will our supporting goals meet our needs?

Collectively, do our supporting goals provide all the stepping stones we need for achieving our goal? Are our supporting goals sufficiently well-defined for us to be confident of this? Request the stakeholders of supporting goals to modify them as necessary to meet our needs. If necessary, create new supporting goals.

•Do we have sound risk management?

Assuming that all our supporting goals deliver as promised, have we identified all risks that could prevent achievement of our own goal? Have we properly assessed the severity of all risks and identified avoidance and mitigation measures so as to achieve an acceptable adjusted severity? See Risk Management.

2.For the map as a whole, repeatedly ask three questions and take any actions that are needed to obtain satisfactory answers:

•Is the map valid?

As the map is steadily clarified, both the change paths and the end business benefits must emerge as entirely valid. Ultimately the stakeholders must agree that

these are the business benefits we are seeking, and the greatest benefits that we can realistically seek at this point, and this map represents our best approach to realising those benefits.

•Is the map complete?

The map must be complete in two distinct senses. First, the top-level goals must genuinely show the final business benefits that are sought, not just some intermediate step towards achieving those benefits. And second, the map must include all the goals necessary to effect the change and ensure that the benefits will actually be realised and harvested.

•Is the map aligned?

Do all goals ‘pull in the same direction’ towards achievement of the business benefits, and do all supporting goals meet the needs of their supported goals?

3.If appropriate, at any time, make the collective decision that the change initiative should be abandoned. In this case, explore the two questions of what have we learned? and where do we go from here?

4.Once the stakeholders are satisfied that the map is sufficiently precise and detailed—and that it is valid, complete and aligned—proceed to the Delivery phase.
