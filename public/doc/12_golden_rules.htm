12 Golden Rules

Think states, not activities

Be sparing with top-level goals

Ideally no more than a dozen—and certainly not 50 or 100! Detail should be in sub-goals and factors, not the top-level goals. It should be possible to grasp the top-level structure of a map with little more than a glance.

Participate and take ownership

Invest time and effort

Pelorus is not ‘an easy ride’. It demands serious time, effort and commitment.

Match responsibility with authority

Stakeholders who are given responsibility must have corresponding authority to discharge that responsibility.

Err on the side of too much detail rather than too little

Wherever there is any doubt, provide three-valued factor targets rather than single-valued, and advanced-level risk definitions rather than basic-level descriptions. Experience suggests that effort invested in elaborating detail is very rarely wasted.

Keep the map at centre stage

Engage other stakeholders

All stakeholders are responsible for both identifying other stakeholders and encouraging their active participation.

Keep the conversations going

Ask dumb questions

More precisely, in Pelorus there are no dumb questions. Or, more precisely still, the only dumb question is the important one that was never asked.

Remember that the map is always provisional

“The best we can do given our current understanding, but always subject to change in the light of new learning and new understanding”.

Learn, adjust and converge

Since the map is always provisional, the final destination cannot be fully known in advance. Ultimately, the stakeholders do not arrive at a place that was fixed from the outset, but rather at one where they are happy to be in the light of what they have learned along the way.
