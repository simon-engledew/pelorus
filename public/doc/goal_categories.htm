Goal categories

Note: While use of advanced factor and risk definitions is encouraged, goal categories should be used sparingly and only after careful consideration. Maps can be created and delivered without ever using goal categories.

At any given time, a goal is in one of four categories: ideal, objective, aim or maintain. These categories are dynamic—during the life of the map, a goal will typically move from one category to another. The default category is objective.

An ideal goal is one that the stakeholders do not aim to achieve during the life of the map. Its purpose is solely to provide a context for other goals in the map—“if we achieve these goals, then they will take us towards our agreed ideal(s)”.

Normally the factors of an ideal goal are given target values—since this is how the ideal is defined—but no predicted/actual values. Equally, an ideal normally has no defined risks.

An objective is a normal goal that the stakeholders are seeking to achieve during the life of the map.

An aim is a goal that the stakeholders are pursuing as a priority, without needing to await the achievement of any other goals.

A maintain goal is one where the stakeholders are seeking only to maintain the current values of all factors, not to improve them. This category is used in two situations:

•when the goal has been achieved

•when the goal has not been achieved but efforts to do so have been abandoned

In some cases, maintaining the current values of all factors will require on-going effort. In other cases, the goal will be ‘stable’ and no further effort will be required.

The category of a goal is indicated by prefixing the goal name with a single capital letter enclosed in square brackets: I for ideal, A for aim, and M for maintain. The default category, objective, is not explicitly indicated—the goal name has no prefix.

During the life of a map, any goal that is an ideal will (normally) always remain an ideal. However, other goals may switch category, perhaps more than once. A ‘typical’ trajectory is objective to aim to maintain.

In a well-formed map:

•any sub-goal or supporting goal of an aim is either an aim or maintain

•any sub-goal or supporting goal of an objective is either an objective, an aim or maintain.

Where goal categories are used, it is no longer the head goals of the map that represent the business benefits of the change, but rather the head objectives (or aims)—that is, those goals whose only supported goals are ideals.
