The traffic-light system

Most elements of a map have a traffic-light status—one of red, amber or green. Broadly:

•green indicates OK

•amber indicates a possible problem that may need attention

•red indicates a definite problem that probably needs action.

Traffic-light statuses are often calculated as the worst case of some set, by applying the following rules in order:

•if any member of the set is red then the worst case is red

•if any member of the set is amber then the worst case is amber

•otherwise the worst case is green.

Within a goal, the various sub-goals, factors, and risks all have their own traffic-light status, as does the discussion of the goal—see the traffic-light status summary. These statuses then propagate to generate a local status for the goal as a whole, using worst case rules.

In addition to its local status, a goal also has a supported status which is propagated from all its supporting goals and is the worst case of all those goals.

The overall status of a goal is then the worst case of its local status and its supported status. Thus, when any goal is amber or red, that status propagates to all its supported goals, then to their supported goals, and so on up the map.

For example, at goal Customer Enquiry Process Redesigned in the map shown on the left below:

•the local status is green (because its sub-goals, factors and risks are all green)

•the supported status is red (because goal Call Centre Staff Trained is red)

•the overall status is red (worst case of local status and supported status).

This red status then propagates to the goals New Process Stabilized, Work Reallocated and Business Benefits.

On viewing this map, any stakeholder can immediately see that there is a problem at goal Call Centre Staff Trained. The stakeholder can drill down to find the precise nature of the problem by visiting the goal and examining its jump to table. This shows the status of each part of the goal—its sub-goals, factors, risks and so on. The stakeholder can then jump to the part of the goal that has the problem and drill down one further level to see the details.

When a problem exists, causing parts of the map to have amber of red status, the stakeholders may choose to contain that problem—that is, they aim to resolve the problem within a certain area of the map so that goals outside that area remain unaffected (see the Actions of the Delivery Phase).  In this case, the stakeholders set any goals at the area’s periphery to be non-propagating, so that their traffic-light status does not propagate to supported goals. The resulting map clearly shows the location of the problem and the decision to contain it.
