Concept Phase

Objective

To create a skeleton map of the proposed change and the intended business benefits. This map must show at least:

•the top-level goal network

•the key stakeholders at each goal

•critical factors and their target values

•critical risks and their severity.

The skeleton map must be sufficiently detailed to allow stakeholders to form a broad (but clear) understanding of the change and its benefits and gain confidence that the change is worth pursuing. It is the stakeholders themselves who decide what level of detail is ‘sufficient’.

One possible outcome of the phase is a decision that the change should not be pursued.

Actions

1.Create a new map; give it a name and a brief description indicating its scope and purpose.

2.Edit the stakes in the map so as to be suitable for inheritance by top-level goals—change the holders of the four distinguished stakes and add new stakes as appropriate.

3.Create a first goal in the map; give it a name and a brief description. This goal should reflect the ‘initial concept’ of the change initiative—the original idea that triggers the change.

4.Repeatedly ask four questions:

•Who are the stakeholders in this goal?

Edit existing stakes and add new stakes as appropriate.

•What precisely is this goal?

If necessary, edit the goal name and/or description to clarify the stakeholders’ improved understanding. As appropriate, create a small number of key factors, and perhaps sub-goals, to further define the goal. A key factor may be either a results factor or a resources factor.

•How will we achieve this goal?

What supporting goals does it require? Create new goals and links as necessary.

•Why do we want to achieve this goal?

What goals will it support? Create new goals and links as necessary?

5.Repeat action 4 for all the goals in the map.

Note that for some goals the answer to the how? question will be we don’t need any supporting goals—we can reach this goal directly and don’t need any ‘stepping stones’. And for other goals the answer to the why? question will be this goal is not a stepping stone to other goals—it is a desirable destination in its own right. These will be tail and head goals in the map, respectively.

6.For each goal in the map, conduct an initial risk identification exercise with the aim of quickly identifying any ‘big’ risks. Add identified risks to the goal. Give each risk a name, a description and a traffic-light status.

7.For any risk that is amber or red, try to identify avoidance and/or mitigation measures so as to reduce the severity of the risk to a level where it is green.

8.Continue to populate the map until stakeholders agree that it is sufficiently complete and precise to give them a clear understanding of (i) the nature, scope and structure of the proposed change; (ii) the nature and scale of the business benefits, and (iii) the nature and scale of the risks.

9.Make a collective decision (all stakeholders) on whether to take the map forward to the Definition phase or to abandon the change initiative at this Concept stage.

10.If the decision is to abandon, explore the two questions of what have we learned? and where do we go from here?

Notes

At every goal, the who?, what?, how? and why? questions must be asked repeatedly. The answers to these questions can change as knowledge increases and changes are made to other goals in the map.

During the Concept phase it can sometimes be appropriate to build two or more skeleton maps, all based on the same initial concept of the change but ‘building out’ from that concept in different ways, resulting in different change paths, different business benefits and/or different risks. The stakeholders then select just one of these alternative maps to take forward to the Definition phase—though they may of course still decide that none of the maps looks promising and that the change initiative should be abandoned.
